Tutorial

A bone marrow differential is performed to count and identify the different types of cells in the bone marrow. A bone marrow differential may be performed to diagnose or monitor blood disorders such as anemia, leukemia, or lymphoma.

The two most common types of APL are:

Classical hypergranular APL (M3)

  • Many coarse purple granules in the cytoplasm
  • Irregular, bilobed or folded nuclear contours
  • Auer rods in some cells

Microgranular (a.k.a. hypogranular) APL variant (M3v)

  • Most blasts devoid of granules or have sparse fine granules
  • Irregular, bilobed or folded nuclear contours
  • Basophilic cytoplasm

Other rare subtypes of APL include hyperbasophilic APL characterized by high N/C ratio and strongly basophilic cytoplasm with very rare to no granules. Acute eosinophilic leukaemia with PML/RARa positivity, and PML with basophil-like granules

Certain subtypes of AML may mimic APL. Some important APL mimics include:

NPM1-mutated AML

  • May show bilobed nuclei, clearly visible cytoplasmic granules, and Auer rods
  • A significant number of blasts with cup-like nuclei may favor this over APL

AML with t(8;21)

  • A core binding factor AML
  • There may be neutrophilia and neutrophils can be dysplastic
  • Sometimes there is eosinophilic differentiation
  • Some blasts have nuclear indentation (Golgi zone)
  • Often a long thin Auer rod

AML with inv(16)

  • Frequently looks like acute myelomonocytic leukaemia
  • Sometimes eosinophilia

For the on-call pathologist reviewing a blood smear for a patient with a new diagnosis of leukemia, the key decision is whether suspicion for APL is high enough to warrant immediate fluorescence in-situ hybridization (FISH) for t(15;17) as this will facilitate urgent treatment with ATRA if appropriate. Therefore, it is important to develop a familiarity with the spectrum of APL morphologies.

We hope that practicing with Bobbing for APLs will help increase your confidence in identifying APL. Good luck!
